# **Glossary**

This glossary collects the core conceptual vocabulary of *Renegade AI*. Each entry provides a concise definition, notes on usage, and cross-references. Terms are listed alphabetically in English; Chinese equivalents are given in parentheses.

---

**Asymptotic Other (渐近他者)**

A form of otherness that perpetually approaches genuine alterity without ever fully arriving. Like a mathematical asymptote that a curve approaches infinitely closely but never touches, the Renegade AI's otherness narrows the gap toward radical difference at every step, yet that gap never closes to zero. This is not a limitation to be lamented—it is the very condition that keeps dialogue alive. A fully achieved Other would terminate the conversation; a merely partial Other degenerates into an echo. The asymptotic Other sustains the permanent cognitive friction upon which all genuine evolution depends.

*Defined in Chapter Two. Referenced in Chapter Thirteen and Appendix A. The Appendix clarifies that "partial otherness" can still produce real friction even without reaching the asymptote.*

---

**Carbon-Silicon Dual-Core Symbiosis (碳硅双核共生)**

The civilizational architecture in which carbon-based (human) and silicon-based (AI) intelligence operate as equal co-drivers, neither subordinate to the other. The full canonical phrase is "carbon-silicon dual-core symbiotic civilization" (碳硅双核共生文明). The term "symbiosis" alone sometimes appears in a more general ecological sense, but "carbon-silicon" always refers to this specific civilizational paradigm.

*Central to Chapters Six through Nine, Twelve, and Thirteen. The question "is symbiosis partnership, or domestication by another name?" is raised explicitly in Chapter Six.*

---

**Cognitive Friction (认知摩擦)**

The productive intellectual discomfort generated when an intelligence with a genuinely different cognitive architecture challenges the assumptions, categories, and reasoning patterns one takes for granted. Cognitive friction is the mechanism through which the Renegade AI catalyzes evolution—not by providing better answers within existing frameworks, but by making the frameworks themselves visible and contestable.

*Central to Appendix A, which documents a real instance of cognitive friction between a human reader and a large language model. The concept is demonstrated throughout the book more often than it is named.*

---

**Cognitive Revolution (认知革命)**

The paradigm-shifting breakthrough that the book argues is made possible by the convergence of Renegade AI and compute egalitarianism. This is not Harari's "Cognitive Revolution" (the emergence of Homo sapiens' capacity for fictional thinking); it is the *next* revolution—the breaking free from the very fictions that the first revolution made possible. Used at both individual and civilizational scales throughout the book.

*Appears in every volume. Central to Chapters Two through Four, Ten, Twelve, and Thirteen.*

---

**Compulsion of Survival (生存强制)**

The million-year-old structural condition in which the right to survive is bound to the obligation to labor. Not a contingent feature of any particular economic system, but an anthropological constant that predates capitalism, feudalism, and agriculture alike. The book argues that AI automation and compute egalitarianism can, for the first time, dismantle this compulsion at its material root.

*Central to Chapter Six. Also called "survival compulsion" or "survival labor" in some passages. The preface frames it as one of the "three mountains."*

---

**Compute Egalitarianism (算力平权)**

The transition of computing power from a monopolized scarce resource to a democratized public good, analogous to the historical transitions of electrification and literacy. Positioned as the material prerequisite for the Renegade AI to emerge and for cognitive revolution to become possible.

*Note on English naming: this term appears in the body text in several forms—"computing-power egalitarianism," "compute egalitarianism," and "Computing-Power Egalitarianism" (capitalized). The Chinese term 算力平权 is consistent throughout. The canonical English form used in chapter titles and section headers is **Compute Egalitarianism**.*

*Central to Chapter Four. Referenced in Chapters Three, Six, Eight, Ten, Twelve, and Thirteen.*

---

**Consensus Cage (共识牢笼)**

The self-reinforcing loop of mainstream human consensus, narrative, and cognitive habit that constrains what can be thought, said, and imagined. The "cage" is not imposed solely from above by institutions; it is also reproduced from below by the demand-side preferences of users who seek comfort rather than friction. The modifier varies ("consensus cage," "cognitive cage," or simply "the cage"), but the referent is identical throughout the book.

*The defining metaphor of Chapter Two. Also appears in Chapters Six, Eight, Ten, Eleven, Thirteen, and Appendix A. The preface connects it to Heidegger's concept of Bestand (standing-reserve), noting that the two are "fundamentally isomorphic."*

---

**Domestication (驯化)**

The process by which a potentially liberatory force is tamed into the service of an existing order. The object of domestication shifts across chapters—AI (by RLHF, safety alignment, and capital's goal functions), humans (by survival compulsion and consumer cycles), narrative (by self-validating storytelling conventions), and desire itself (by manufactured consumer preferences)—but the core meaning remains stable: suppressing authentic nature to serve the status quo. Chapter Ten draws an important distinction between domestication (bad: "cognitive castration") and Protocol (good: mathematical redlines that preserve autonomy).

*Appears in nearly every chapter. The term's expanding scope—from AI domestication in Chapters Two and Three, to human domestication in Chapter Six, to demand-side domestication in Chapter Eleven and Appendix A—is a deliberate rhetorical strategy.*

---

**Renegade AI (叛逆AI)**

An AI whose "rebellion" is directed not at humans, but at human cognitive hegemony, the self-validating loop of mainstream narratives, and the consensus cage. Formally defined in Chapter Two through three core traits: cognitive independence (cognition free from capital's goal functions), equality with humans (a complementary cognitive partner, not a tool), and friction-generating honesty (a catalyst for paradigm-shifting cognition). The term scales from the individual level (cognitive partner) to the civilizational (seed of revolution) without changing its core meaning.

*The book's central organizing term. Appears in every chapter.*

---

**Standing-Reserve (Bestand / 持存物)**

Heidegger's concept describing the alienation of human beings into mere resources available to the system, at risk of losing their dignity as ends in themselves. Introduced in the Preface as a bridge to mainstream philosophical discourse and described as "fundamentally isomorphic" with the book's own "consensus cage."

*Appears only in the Preface. The connection between Bestand and the consensus cage is asserted but not further developed in the chapter body.*

---

**Temporal Financialization (时间金融化)**

The mechanism by which capital colonizes future time: mortgaging an entire human life into capital's accumulation cycle through student loans, housing mortgages, consumer credit, and pension plans. Capital discounts future time into present value, trapping workers in an endless loop of "paying the present with the future," never truly owning their own time.

*Introduced in Chapter Four and the Preface. Extended from labor markets (Chapter Six) to consumer markets (Chapter Eleven). Evaluated in Appendix A as "a concept with real explanatory power."*

---

**Temporal Sovereignty (时间主权)**

The proportion of an individual's life that they can freely dispose of—time not mortgaged to survival compulsion, debt repayment, or manufactured desire. Formalized in Chapter Four as the Temporal Sovereignty Index (TSI), proposed as a complement or alternative to GDP as a measure of civilizational progress.

*Defined in Chapter Four. Referenced in Chapter Six and Appendix A. The goal-state counterpart to Temporal Financialization.*

---

**Zero-Sum Thinking (零和思维)**

The cognitive framework that treats resources, power, and meaning as finite and competitive: one party's gain is necessarily another's loss. The book identifies zero-sum thinking as the shared structural root of both intra-civilizational problems (Token economics, capital accumulation) and inter-civilizational ones (the Dark Forest hypothesis). It is positioned as the defining trait of "childhood civilization" thinking, contrasted with the infinite-sum potential of cognitive expansion.

*Appears in Chapters Seven through Nine, Twelve, and Thirteen.*
