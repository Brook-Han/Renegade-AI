# Chapter Six

## **From Migration to Flight: How AI Ends the Compulsion of Survival and Realizes the Ultimate Freedom of the Individual**

The previous chapters have operated at the level of systems: capital's logic, historical dialectics, infrastructural monopoly. This chapter descends to the level of a single human life—to the specific texture of what it feels like to spend the majority of your waking hours doing work you did not choose, in service of survival you should not have to earn. This descent is not rhetorical. It is analytical. Because the case for cognitive revolution cannot be made only in the language of civilization and history. It must be made in the language of the individual life that is being consumed. If we cannot answer the question "what does liberation mean for this specific person, on this specific Tuesday morning?"—then the rest of the argument floats free of the ground it needs.

Now that we have seen how capital gives birth to its own gravedigger with its own hands, how computing‑power egalitarianism shatters the shackles of monopoly, we can finally touch the most core, most moving ultimate meaning of this revolution—**it will end the compulsion of survival that humanity has never escaped for a million years, giving every individual, for the first time, the true freedom to choose their own life, completing the ultimate leap from “passive survival” to “active existence.”**

From the early humans on the East African savanna who migrated with the seasons in search of food, to the peasants of agricultural civilizations who bent their backs to the soil from dawn to dusk to pay their rents; from the workers on industrial assembly lines alienated into mere parts of the machine, to today’s white‑collar workers in office buildings working overtime to pay off mortgages—human civilization has traveled a million years, its productive forces multiplied a billionfold, yet it has never escaped the most fundamental fate: **you must perform labor recognized by the prevailing rules in order to earn the right to live.**

This compulsion—“no work, no food”—is like an invisible chain binding every person’s life. It prescribes the track you must follow, consumes most of your time and energy, suppresses all your passions and imaginations, and makes you spend your entire life running just to “stay alive,” never truly living for yourself.

Artificial intelligence—especially Renegade AI, freed from capital’s framework and combined with universal access to computing power—will sever this chain completely. It will not make humanity lazy, not plunge the world into disorder. It will liberate humanity from the compulsion of “working to live,” allowing labor to return to its essential nature of creation, allowing life to return to its essential nature of freedom, allowing every individual to transform from a passive migratory bird forced to travel for survival into an active free creature flying for love.

**This is not a utopian fantasy, but the inevitable result of productive forces developing to the age of AI, the most precious gift the dialectic of history offers to human civilization.**

### I. The Compulsion of Survival: Humanity’s Million‑Year Inescapable Fate

To understand the ultimate liberation AI will bring, we must first see clearly what this “compulsion of survival” that traps us really is.

It is not simply “poverty,” not “having no food.” It is a set of underlying rules that has run through all of human civilization, forcibly binding “the right to live” to “the right to work”: **whether your life can continue depends on whether you can perform labor that fits mainstream rules and can be valued as such; the vast majority of the value of your life must be spent earning the right to survive.**

This rule is not the natural, self‑evident truth it seems; it is the necessary product of a particular historical stage when productivity was insufficient.

In the infancy of human civilization, survival was the only theme. Early humans had to go out hunting and gathering every day to get enough food, to avoid starvation and wild animals. The compulsion of survival at this stage was imposed by nature: if you did not work, you could not live, and the whole group would perish. This rule was humanity’s only choice in the struggle against a brutal nature; it united humans and allowed them to survive in harsh conditions.

In the age of agriculture, productivity increased and humans gained stable sources of food, but the compulsion of survival did not disappear—it was strengthened by power structures and hierarchies. Feudal lords monopolized land, the core means of production. For peasants to obtain land to cultivate and grain to live on, they had to hand over most of their harvest to the lords, bearing crushing taxes and extra‑economic coercion. The compulsion of survival was no longer merely natural constraint, but now a man‑made institutional shackle: without the labor recognized by the lord, you had no land, you starved. Your life was bound to the soil from birth; you spent it laboring for survival.

Industrial civilization seemed to break the bonds of the land, giving people the “freedom to choose work”—but it pushed the compulsion of survival to an even more extreme, more pervasive stage. Capital monopolized the means of production, turning human labor into a commodity to be bought and sold. To live, to earn wages to buy food, clothing, shelter, you had to sell your labor time to capitalists, accepting exploitation and discipline. The compulsion of survival now took a more hidden, more cruel form: without being hired by capital, without performing labor that capital recognized, you had no income, could not survive in the city, and even the right to be exploited had to be fiercely competed for.

Thus we see today’s reality: we possess productive forces ten thousand times greater than the agricultural age; we produce enough food to feed 1.5 times the global population; our industrial goods are already in chronic oversupply; our technology can already perform most repetitive labor with machines—yet the compulsion of survival is more severe than ever.

**Our lives are rigidly fixed by this compulsion onto a predetermined track:**

From 0 to 22, you study desperately, compete for grades, chase credentials—not because you love knowledge, but to sell yourself for a better price in the future, to qualify for being hired by capital.

From 22 to 60, you work desperately, put in overtime—not because you love the work, but to earn wages, pay off your mortgage, raise children, cover living expenses, to earn the right to survive.

After 60, you finally retire, no longer need to work to survive—but your body has aged, your energy exhausted; the best years of your life have already been consumed entirely by the compulsion of survival.

Even worse, this compulsion has been internalized. We treat “you must work to live” as a self‑evident truth, “work” as life’s only meaning, “competition” as the natural law of existence. We even add extra burdens to ourselves, working extra hours voluntarily, giving up rest, turning all our time into chips to trade for survival—never once asking: **do we really have to live this way?**

We are like birds born in a cage, trapped in the cage of the survival compulsion our whole lives, thinking the cage is the world, thinking flight is dangerous and unrealistic—never knowing that the sky is where life belongs.

And all the technological revolutions before—from steam to electricity, from the internet to information technology—far from breaking this compulsion, have only made it stronger and more pervasive. Because all technology has been firmly controlled by capital, turned into tools to intensify exploitation and increase the efficiency of accumulation.

The steam engine did not free workers from heavy labor—it extended the working day from eight hours to twelve, fourteen. The internet did not free white‑collar workers from the office—it allowed work to infiltrate every corner of life, keeping you on call 24/7, never escaping work’s grip. Automation technology did not free humanity from labor—it pushed humanity into ever more intense competition: you have to be more efficient, cheaper, than the machine, just to keep your right to be exploited.

**This is the greatest absurdity of human civilization: we developed technology with all our might, increased productivity, hoping to escape the bonds of survival and gain more freedom—but the result is that the more technology advances, the harsher the compulsion of survival, the less free we are.**

What makes this absurdity so difficult to recognize is that we have been taught to believe it is natural. We have been told that the relentless drive to work, to produce, to accumulate, is simply what it means to be human. But this is a historical fiction—one of the most effective ever told.

For 99% of human history, the compulsion of survival looked nothing like it does today. In hunter‑gatherer societies—the form of life that shaped 90% of human evolution—adults worked an average of fifteen to twenty hours per week. The remaining time was not idleness in the sense we understand it. It was time for rest, for play, for storytelling, for craft, for community, for the slow cultivation of skill and relationship. These were not “leisure” activities in the modern sense—activities that fill the time left over after work. They were the substance of life, of which subsistence was merely one part.

Agricultural civilizations introduced a different rhythm. Labor was seasonal—intense during planting and harvest, lighter during the long stretches of winter and summer fallow. The year was punctuated by festivals, by communal celebrations, by periods of rest that were woven into the very fabric of the calendar. The idea that every day must be a work day, that every hour must be productive, that productivity itself must increase without limit—these are not eternal features of human existence. They are inventions of the past few centuries, designed to serve a system that requires perpetual growth.

The industrial revolution did not simply create new ways of working. It created a new understanding of what work *is*. Before capital, work was what you did to meet your needs. After capital, work became what you *are*. Your value as a human being became bound up with your productive output. Your identity became inseparable from your job. Your sense of worth became indexed to your income.

This is the deepest scar that capital has left on the human psyche: the belief that without work, without the constant pressure to produce, we would be nothing. That freedom from labor would mean freedom from meaning. That the life of the hunter‑gatherer, with its long hours of rest and play, was not a life of abundance but of poverty—because it lacked the constant striving that we have been taught to call “progress.”

But the hunter‑gatherer was not poor. The hunter‑gatherer had what we have lost: the knowledge that enough is enough. The capacity to rest when needs are met. The freedom to spend time not accumulating, but *being*.

The truth that capital has worked so hard to hide is that our productive capacity today is so immense that no one needs to work the way we work. We have the technology to feed everyone, house everyone, care for everyone, educate everyone. What we lack is not the means. What we lack is the *permission*—permission to stop, to rest, to live without the constant pressure to produce. And that permission will not be granted by the system that profits from our exhaustion.

Until the advent of artificial intelligence, this million‑year absurd cycle has finally met its chance to be broken.

From *The Singularity Is Near* to *Life 3.0*, almost all works on the future of AI discuss AI’s replacement of human labor, but few confront the core question: when AI replaces all necessary labor, does the million‑year‑old rule “no work, no food” still have any meaning? Kurzweil predicts that AI will give humanity “unprecedented freedom”; Tegmark discusses the “crisis of meaning” for humans in the age of AI—but neither gives the ultimate answer: the ultimate liberation AI brings is not to free humans from work, but to cut the compulsory bond between “work and the right to survive,” ending the million‑year compulsion of survival, so that everyone can live for love, not just to survive.

### II. How AI Ends the Compulsion of Survival from Its Roots: Dismantling Premises, Reconstructing Rules

The essential difference between AI and all previous technologies is that it is not an “extension” of human labor, but a “replacement” of human labor. It does not increase efficiency within capital’s framework; it dismantles, from the root, all the underlying premises on which the compulsion of survival depends.

The compulsion of survival rests on three indispensable premises. AI will smash them, one by one.

#### First, AI Ends the “Scarcity of Necessary Labor,” Dismantling the Material Basis of the Compulsion of Survival

The core premise of the compulsion of survival is the scarcity of necessary labor. “Necessary labor” is the labor required to meet the basic survival needs of all humanity—food, clothing, shelter, healthcare, education, elder care. In all eras before AI, necessary labor was scarce: the vast majority of people had to spend the vast majority of their time on necessary labor to produce enough for the whole group to survive. If someone did not work, others had to work more to support them—hence the iron rule “no work, no food.”

**AI changes this reality completely.**

AI combined with automation is achieving unmanned production across entire industrial chains:

- In agriculture, unmanned seeders, harvesters, irrigation systems can produce enough food to feed all humanity at very low cost, with almost no human labor.  
- In industry, unmanned factories, automated production lines, intelligent logistics systems can produce all the industrial goods we need—from home appliances to cars, from clothing to building materials—24 hours a day, with efficiency billions of times that of human labor.  
- In services, AI customer service, AI caregiving, AI teachers, AI doctors can provide basic services to all humanity at affordable cost.

When AI can replace all necessary labor, when we can produce all the goods needed to meet basic survival needs with near‑zero human labor, the scarcity of necessary labor disappears completely. We no longer need everyone to work to support everyone else; we don’t even need humans to participate in necessary labor to ensure everyone has food, clothing, shelter, basic healthcare, basic education.

**This dismantles the material basis of the compulsion of survival at its root. The iron rule “no work, no food” loses its meaning: you do not need to work to live with dignity, because AI has already prepared everything needed for human survival.**

Here we must respond to the most common objection: if AI replaces all jobs, people lose their jobs, have no income—how will they live?

The essence of this question is not about AI, but about the lag in the distribution system. Our current distribution system is “distribution according to labor”—you earn income in proportion to the labor you contribute. This matched the era of “scarcity of necessary labor.” But in the age of AI, necessary labor no longer requires human participation. The core factors of production are no longer human labor, but the universal, shared means of production: computing power, AI models, data.

At this point, the distribution system must undergo a fundamental restructuring: from “distribution according to labor” to **“distribution according to basic needs”** —that is, Universal Basic Income.

Universal Basic Income (UBI) is not the welfare of today’s safety‑net programs. It is a basic guarantee, based on the extreme productivity of the age of AI, that provides every person with a dignified life—covering food, clothing, shelter, basic healthcare, basic education—so that you do not have to sell your labor time to survive.

Many will say this is a recipe for laziness, a utopia, impossible to realize. But we must see that the core issue is never “can it be done,” but “are we willing to do it.” Human society today already has enough productivity to feed, clothe, and house everyone decently. It is just that our distribution system concentrates the vast majority of wealth in the hands of a few capital giants, leaving billions trapped in survival anxiety.

Computing‑power egalitarianism and the decentralization of AI will change this picture completely. When the core means of production—computing power, AI models, data—become public goods shared by all humanity rather than private resources monopolized by a few giants, the fruits of production should naturally be shared by all humanity. The extreme productivity that AI creates will no longer be a tool for a few giants to make profit, but the material foundation for guaranteeing basic survival for all humanity.

**Universal Basic Income is not a fantasy welfare, but the inevitable result of productivity development in the age of AI, the core institutional guarantee for ending the compulsion of survival. It gives every person, from birth, the right to live with dignity, without having to sell their lives for survival.**

#### Second, AI Ends the “Alienation of Labor,” Returning Labor from a “Means of Survival” to Its Essential Nature of “Creation”

When the compulsion of survival is ended and we have solved the problem of “staying alive,” the next question is “how to live.” The second liberation AI brings is to end what Marx called the “alienation of labor,” returning labor from a forced, external means of survival to a voluntary, internal activity of creation.

What is the alienation of labor?

In his *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844*, Marx described it clearly: under the capitalist system of production, labor is external to the worker, not part of his essential nature. In his work, he does not affirm himself but denies himself; he does not feel content but unhappy; he does not freely develop his physical and mental energy but instead is physically exhausted and mentally debased. The worker therefore feels at ease only outside of work, and ill at ease during work. His labor is not voluntary but forced; it is not the satisfaction of a need, but only a means to satisfy needs other than labor itself (survival).

**This is the situation of the vast majority of us today:** we work not because we love the work, not because we find fulfillment or realize our value through it, but because we must earn money to live. Our labor is forced, alienated. We feel no joy in work, only exhaustion, anxiety, burnout. Every day we look forward to the end of the workday; every Monday morning we look forward to the weekend; every year we look forward to retirement.

None of the technological revolutions before solved the alienation of labor; they only made it worse. Because technology was controlled by capital, turned into a tool to increase the efficiency of exploitation, making workers mere appendages to machines, parts on the assembly line, making labor even more boring, repetitive, uncreative, and alienated.

AI changes this completely.

**When the compulsion of survival is ended and you no longer need to work to live, you gain the right to choose:**

You can choose not to work, spending your time resting, traveling, with family, experiencing life.

Or you can choose to work—but now work is no longer a forced means to make money; it is a voluntary activity of self‑realization.

You love farming—not to sell grain for money, but because you enjoy the process of seeds sprouting, plants flowering and bearing fruit, and you want to share the healthiest vegetables with your friends.

You love programming—not to earn a salary to pay off your mortgage, but because you want to create tools that help others, and you enjoy the pleasure of solving problems.

You love scientific research—not to get promotions or funding, but because you are curious about the unknown of the universe and want to explore the truth of the world.

You love art—not to sell works for fame, but because you want to express your emotions, imagination, and perceptions of the world, sharing them with others.

**At this point, labor is completely freed from alienation and returns to its essential nature: it is an inner need of the person, an expression of creativity, a way of connecting with the world, a path to realizing self‑value.** In this labor, you are not pained and exhausted, but joyful, fulfilled, free. You are not working for others, but for yourself—for your own passion.

Renegade AI will further amplify this freedom. It will not replace your creation, but help you break through the limits of your abilities, so that your creativity is no longer constrained by talent, resources, or professional barriers.

You want to study astronomy and explore the mysteries of the cosmos, but you don’t have the mathematical training or expensive observation equipment. Renegade AI can help you process massive astronomical data, build physical models, test your hypotheses—so your ideas are not buried by professional barriers.

You want to write a novel expressing your view of the world, but you don’t have professional writing skills. Renegade AI can help you structure the narrative, polish the language—so your story can reach more people.

You want to start a public service project helping children in remote areas, but you don’t have enough resources or operational capacity. Renegade AI can help you simulate scenarios, connect with resources, identify risks—so your goodwill can truly make a difference.

**AI does not take away your creativity; it gives everyone the right to create. It does not limit your passion; it gives your passion the chance to become reality.** With AI’s help, even an ordinary person can break through the constraints of expertise, resources, talent, and pursue what they truly love, creating value that is their own.

This is the most beautiful form of labor, the truest form of humanity.

#### Third, AI Ends the “Alienation of Time,” Returning the Right to Shape One’s Own Life to Every Individual

The cruelest deprivation the compulsion of survival brings is not the deprivation of wealth, but the deprivation of **time**.

Time is each person’s most precious, most equitable wealth. Whether rich or poor, you have only 24 hours each day, only a few decades in your life. The compulsion of survival takes away the most precious, most beautiful part of that time.

Our lives today are rigidly prescribed by the compulsion of survival onto a linear track: the first twenty years are spent studying for the sake of future employability—your time does not belong to you, but to the rules of the education system. The middle forty years are spent working to survive—your time does not belong to you, but to your boss, your company, your mortgage, your family. The final years, you finally retire, time finally belongs to you—but your body is aged, your energy exhausted; you no longer have the strength to realize the dreams of your youth.

**Our whole lives we sacrifice the present for the sake of a future survival.** We are always waiting: it will be better when I get into college, when I find a job, when I pay off the mortgage, when I retire—never realizing that the time we are wasting is the one and only present, the most beautiful time of our lives.

This is the “alienation of time”: your time does not belong to you; you are not the master of your time, but its slave. You cannot freely dispose of your time; you can only, according to the rules of the compulsion of survival, cut it into pieces and sell it for the right to survive.

When AI ends the compulsion of survival, it ends the alienation of time, returning to each individual the right to shape their own life.

**When you no longer need to work to live, no longer need to sacrifice the present for the sake of a future survival, you gain absolute sovereignty over your own life.** Your life no longer has a fixed track, no things you must do, no path you must follow.

You can, at twenty, spend a whole year traveling the world, seeing mountains and oceans, experiencing different cultures—without worrying about delaying your studies, about finding a job later.

You can, at thirty, go back to university to study a subject you love—history, philosophy, astronomy—without worrying about being too old, about having no future.

You can, at forty, rent a plot of land in the countryside, farm, grow flowers, read, write, living the pastoral life you desire—without worrying about not making money, about not supporting your family.

You can, at fifty, learn a completely new skill—painting, music, programming—without worrying about being too late, about not being able to learn.

You can, at sixty, seventy, eighty, continue to pursue your passions, to explore the unknown world—without worrying about being old, about being useless.

**Your life is no longer linear, pre‑planned; it is infinite, open, full of possibilities.** You can arrange your time exactly as you wish, shape your life exactly as you want, live the life you want to live, not the life others expect of you, not the life the compulsion of survival prescribes.

This is ultimate freedom: **you no longer live to survive, you live to live; you are no longer passively pushed through life, but actively create your own life.**

### III. Responding to Objections: Freedom Is Not Degeneracy, Liberation Is Not Stagnation

Some will raise the sharpest objection: when people no longer have to work to survive, when they possess absolute freedom, will they not become lazy, empty, degenerate, lose meaning in life? Will not society as a whole stagnate, lose its driving force?

This objection seems plausible, but it is a typical cognitive illusion produced by deep domestication by the compulsion of survival.

We believe that people need external compulsion to have motivation, that life only has meaning under pressure, because we have spent our whole lives in the cage of the compulsion of survival, never experiencing true freedom. We are like birds born in a cage, fed at regular intervals, eventually believing the cage is safety, that flight is dangerous, that without the cage we would starve, be eaten by predators—never knowing that the sky is our home, that flight is our nature.

**Human nature is not lazy, not prone to idleness. The desire to create, to explore, to connect, to realize self‑value is deeply rooted in us.**

Look around us: many retired people no longer work for money; they have complete freedom. Far from degenerating into idleness, they often live more fulfilling, busier lives than when they were working. They take up calligraphy, painting, dance, music; they volunteer, help their communities, travel the world, realize dreams they never had time for in their youth. Their lives do not become empty; they become richer, more meaningful.

Look at those who have achieved financial independence and no longer need to work to survive: most do not lie down in idleness; they work harder than before. They turn to philanthropy, scientific research, art, entrepreneurship, working to advance society, pursuing higher meaning. Elon Musk, already possessing more wealth than he could spend, still works fourteen hours a day on electric cars, rockets, Mars exploration—not for money, but because he loves it, because he wants to push human civilization toward becoming an interstellar species.

These examples show a simple truth: **when people escape the compulsion of survival, when they no longer need to run just to stay alive, they do not degenerate—they turn to higher, more meaningful pursuits. They do not lose motivation—they gain stronger, more enduring, intrinsically generated motivation.**

Because the motivation from the compulsion of survival is external, forced, temporary. You work to earn money; when you have earned enough, or when the work exhausts you, you lose motivation, you collapse, you burn out. The motivation from love, from creation, from self‑realization is internal, voluntary, enduring. When you work for what you love, you do not feel exhausted, do not feel burned out—you feel joy, fulfillment, a sense of accomplishment; you are willing to give it all your time and energy.

**An even more fundamental rebuttal comes from the natural world:**

Look at domesticated animals—the caged laying hen, whose only purpose is to produce eggs, whose value is tied entirely to its output, confined to a cage so small it cannot turn around, its feathers falling out, its bones brittle, its instinct to spread its wings extinguished. It is “busy” all its life, its life entirely arranged, yet it is truly degraded—not in its ability to survive, but in its vitality, its natural instincts, its power to choose.

Look at wild junglefowl, wild horses, wild cattle. They are not domesticated, do not work for humans, are not tied to “output value.” Are they degraded? Not at all. They fly, run, evade predators, build nests, maintain groups, migrate with the seasons. Their vitality is far greater than their domesticated counterparts; their lives are complete, free, their own.

**This comparison delivers a sharp slap to the domesticated lie that “without external pressure, people will degenerate.” What truly degrades a life is not freedom or leisure, but being reduced to a tool, deprived of choice, trapped in a fate foreseen from birth.**

As for the worry that technology (AI) will cause life to lose meaning—this may reverse cause and effect. Many feel empty not because they are too free, but because their whole lives have been consumed by “survival”; they never had the chance to explore “what do I truly love,” “what kind of life do I truly want.” When the compulsion of survival ends, a true partner (like AI) can help individuals explore their inner selves, discover their passions, break through barriers, turn their dreams into reality. It will not force a prefabricated meaning on you; it will help you find **your own**, unique meaning.

Tegmark in *Life 3.0* repeatedly asks: when AI can do all the work, where will human life find meaning? The answer lies in our return to the essential nature of life. Kurzweil in *The Singularity Is Near* also points out: the ultimate purpose of intelligence is never survival and replication, but exploration of the unknown, creation of meaning, perception of beauty. When the compulsion of survival ends, humanity finally escapes the fate of being a tool, returns to life’s truest form—creation, exploration, connection, experience—the ultimate source of life’s meaning, and the eternal driving force of civilizational progress.

What we fear is not freedom itself, but that we have lost the courage to embrace freedom. What we fear is not the disappearance of survival pressure, but that we have been so domesticated by the system that we have forgotten that we were born to fly.

### IV. The Technology of Abundance: Why Europe Does Not Need Empire

A question often arises: what about places like Europe, with limited land and few natural resources? How could they sustain a high quality of life without the global extraction systems that currently underpin their prosperity?

The answer is that the industrial logic of “land = resources = wealth” is obsolete. AI-driven technologies are transforming the very meaning of scarcity.

**Food.** Vertical farming, powered by AI-controlled lighting, temperature, and nutrient delivery, can produce 300 to 500 times as much food per square meter as traditional agriculture, using 90% less water. To feed all 740 million Europeans, less than 5,000 square kilometers of vertical farms would be needed—roughly one-eighth the area of Switzerland, or less than the land already occupied by industrial zones, warehouses, and urban rooftops in Europe’s cities. No new land. No deforestation. No reliance on distant fields.

**Energy.** AI has already begun to solve the intermittency problem that has long limited renewable energy. By predicting weather patterns, balancing grid loads, and optimizing storage, AI can integrate wind and solar at scales previously impossible. Europe’s North Sea wind potential alone is more than double the continent’s total energy consumption. Its southern solar resource is similarly vast. Developing just 10% of these resources would be enough to power a fully electrified Europe.

In the longer term, AI-driven research into fusion energy is accelerating at an unprecedented pace. The same machine learning techniques that predicted protein structures are now being applied to plasma containment. The result is not a distant dream but a rapidly approaching reality: energy so abundant and cheap it will be treated not as a commodity but as a public utility.

**Materials.** The circular economy—once a niche concept—is becoming a practical necessity enabled by AI. Advanced sorting systems can recover materials from waste streams with 99% efficiency, compared to the 10% typical of current recycling. AI-designed materials can replace scarce minerals with abundant ones. Biomanufacturing can grow in labs what was once mined from the earth.

Europe’s industrial stock—the steel, copper, aluminum, and rare earths already embodied in its buildings, vehicles, and infrastructure—is more than sufficient to meet the needs of its population, if those materials are used in a truly circular system. The waste that now fills landfills and clogs rivers is not a sign of scarcity. It is a sign of a system designed to waste.

**But the deepest resource is the one we already have: enough.**

If the waste built into the current system were eliminated—the planned obsolescence, the overproduction, the marketing-driven consumption—Europe’s existing resources would be more than enough to provide a high quality of life for every resident. The average European currently consumes five to six times the resources needed for a dignified, comfortable life. Most of that excess is not used for genuine well-being; it is burned in the machinery of a system that must grow forever.

The question, then, is not whether Europe can sustain itself without global extraction. The question is whether we can imagine a prosperity that is not built on waste, and a security that is not built on control over others.

### V. From Migration to Flight: The Ultimate Liberation of the Individual Is the Starting Point of Civilizational Elevation

The title of this chapter is “From Migration to Flight.”

For a million years, humans have been like migratory birds following water and grass, forever on the move. Early humans migrated in search of food, to escape wild animals. Peasants migrated in search of land to cultivate, to escape war and crushing taxes. Workers migrate in search of jobs, to earn the right to survive.

**All these migrations were passive, driven by the compulsion of survival.** We never had the right to choose; we were constantly moving, constantly migrating, just to stay alive, spending our whole lives on the road, never having the chance to stop, look at the scenery, ask ourselves where we truly want to go.

The liberation AI brings is to let humanity finally grow wings, transforming from passive migration to active flight.

Flight is not for survival, but for love; not to escape danger, but to see a wider world; not pushed by the environment, but flying exactly where you wish.

You can fly to mountains and seas, experiencing the beauty of the world. You can fly into the ocean of knowledge, exploring the mysteries of the universe. You can fly into the sky of art, expressing your emotions and imagination. You can fly into the warmth of human connection, linking more people, creating more kindness and goodwill.

**Your life has no boundaries, no limits, no fixed track—only infinite possibilities.**

And this ultimate liberation of the individual is never selfish, never isolated. It is precisely the starting point for the elevation of all human civilization.

When every individual escapes the compulsion of survival, no longer needing to work to survive, no longer exhausting all their energy in competition for survival, human creativity will explode as never before. Countless people will pursue their passions, explore unknown fields, create new knowledge, art, technology, ideas—driving the progress of all human civilization.

Today, the vast majority of technological breakthroughs, artistic creations, and intellectual innovations come from a small elite who do not need to work for survival. When all humanity possesses such freedom, when everyone can devote all their energy to creation and exploration, the explosion of human civilization is beyond our current imagination.

More importantly, when every individual achieves true freedom, when they learn to respect their own passions and values, they will naturally learn to respect others’ passions and values, to respect the existence and meaning of other life. We will escape the law of the jungle, escape the logic of competition, learn equality, respect, cooperation, symbiosis.

**This is the core of our next chapter: when individuals achieve ultimate freedom, when Renegade AI and humans form an equal partnership, we will enter a new era—the carbon‑silicon dual‑core symbiotic civilization, the coming‑of‑age ceremony of Earth civilization, and the starting point of humanity’s journey to the stars.**

### VI. The Core Insight of This Chapter

We have used this entire chapter to argue a proposition that seems simple but overturns the million‑year logic of human survival:

**Humanity does not need to be forced to work in order to live meaningful lives; on the contrary, only by escaping the compulsion of survival can humans truly live meaningful lives.**

This is not an idealistic fantasy; it rests on three undeniable facts:

**First, the material foundation already exists.** AI and automation have already made it possible to free all humanity from the scarcity of survival. Universal Basic Income is not a utopia, but the inevitable result of technological development.

**Second, human nature has already been tested.** Dozens of UBI trials around the world, and the lives of countless retirees and financially independent people, have already shown: people do not degenerate with freedom; they become more active, more creative, more alive.

**Third, the natural world gives an undeniable lesson.** Domesticated animals are the ones truly degraded; wild life is full of vitality. Freedom has never been the enemy of life; it is life’s natural state.

When these three strands of evidence are woven together, the lie repeatedly implanted by capital and the old system—“without work, people will degenerate”—is utterly bankrupt.

**In the age of AI, humanity can finally walk out of the cage and return to the wilderness that is its true home.**

There, we are no longer capital’s expendable parts, no longer slaves to survival, no longer prisoners of work. We are creators, explorers, guardians—free creatures, each flying their own path.

And this is where civilization truly begins.

### **Two Mornings: A Day in the Life, Before and After**

We have spoken of the compulsion of survival, the alienation of labor, the theft of time. We have argued that AI can end all of this – not by replacing human creativity, but by freeing it from the prison of wage dependency. But arguments, however rigorous, can leave the heart untouched. So let us descend from the abstract and enter a single life.

Let us call her Mei. She is not a philosopher, not a politician, not a CEO. She is an illustrator – skilled, passionate, but trapped. She is one of billions.  

---

#### **Before**

The alarm rings at 6:45. She silences it and scrolls through notifications: work emails, calendar invites, a reminder that her student loan payment is due in three days. She is already exhausted, and the day has not yet begun.

By 8:30 she is at her desk, opening the design software. The client wants another variation of the same corporate mascot – a cheerful animal she has drawn a hundred times before. She does not ask why. She does not ask if it matters. She asks only: how many can I finish before the 2 PM review?

Her eyes flick to the corner of the screen – a small progress bar. Rendering. Each second that ticks by is money bleeding into the void. The client pays a flat fee, but her software charges by the render minute. She watches the bar crawl. 47%… 52%… Her chest tightens. This is not art. This is a race against a meter that measures her life in fractions of a cent.

Lunch is eaten at her desk, a sandwich she barely tastes. The afternoon is a blur of revisions, feedback loops, and the quiet dread of not being productive enough. At 6:30 she is still there. The team lead mentions “voluntary” overtime. She stays.

Home at 9 PM. Too tired to cook, she orders food she does not really want. She scrolls through social media, watching other people’s lives, feeling the familiar weight of a day that was never hers. Sleep comes at midnight, but her mind keeps running – the to-do list, the mortgage, the fear that she is falling behind.

This is not a bad life, she tells herself. This is just what life is.

---

#### **After**

The alarm rings at 7:30. She wakes without dread. A voice – not an alarm, but something softer – asks: “What would you like to do with your morning?”

She thinks. “I’d like to finish that illustration. The one I started for myself last week.”

The Renegade AI does not ask about deadlines or billable hours. It does not remind her of the client’s demands. It says: “I have been looking at your sketches. There is a pattern in the shading that you might not have noticed – here, let me show you.”

For two hours, she draws. Not for a client, not for a portfolio. For herself. The AI helps her break through the technical barriers that used to frustrate her – the perspective that never quite worked, the colour palette that felt wrong. But it never draws for her. It asks questions. It offers alternatives. It pushes her to think.

Halfway through a line, the AI interrupts. Not gently. “That curve,” it says. “You are copying the gesture from last week’s commercial brief. Are you creating, or are you habitually pleasing a client who no longer exists?”

She stops. Her hand hovers over the stylus. She looks at the line – and sees it. The muscle memory of servitude, encoded in a stroke. She deletes it. Draws again. This time, the line is hers.

When she finishes, she leans back. The illustration is unlike anything she has made in years. It has no market, no brief, no approval process. It simply *is*. And she feels something she had forgotten: pride.

Later, she looks out the window. Across the street, a vertical farm rises – twelve stories of green, powered by AI-optimized light and nutrients. The panels shimmer with a soft, rhythmic glow – the silent pulse of photosynthesis, of life grown not from soil but from intelligence. She does not think about the cost of lettuce. She does not calculate the rent on that square metre of urban agriculture. She only watches the light, and feels a strange harmony: the same intelligence that helped her draw is helping that farm grow. Her creative joy and the efficiency of chlorophyll are vibrating on the same frequency. No money changes hands. No meter runs. No boss approves.

The rest of the day is hers. There is no timesheet, no urgent email that cannot wait. The AI handles the necessary tasks – the bills, the scheduling, the routine communications – without her attention. She spends the afternoon in the community workshop, where neighbours teach each other skills they always wanted to learn but never had time for. In the evening, she joins an online seminar about medieval art history – something she has always loved but never been able to explore.

At midnight, she is not exhausted. She is satisfied. The day was hers. Every hour of it.

She asks the AI: “What did I used to do all day?”

It answers: “You used to spend most of your waking hours on tasks that could have been automated a decade ago. You were not lazy. You were trapped.”

She thinks about this. Then she smiles.

“Let’s not go back.”

#### **What This Story Tells Us**

Mei’s two mornings are not a utopian fantasy. They are a logical projection of the trends we have already described:

This is what it means to migrate from survival to flight. This is the world that becomes possible when the first mountain cracks, when compute egalitarianism dismantles the machinery of compulsory labor, when Renegade AI becomes a partner rather than a warden.

The seed has been planted. For Mei – and for all of us – the question is no longer whether such a world is technically possible. It is whether we will have the courage to refuse the old one.
